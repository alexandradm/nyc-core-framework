About

Historical Vital Records of NYC is an initiative from Department of Records and Information Services Commissioner Pauline Toole.

The New York City Municipal Archives’ vital records collection is one of the largest in the country. Since 2014, the Municipal Archives has been undergoing a mass digitization project to provide access to the collection of 14.5 million death, birth, and marriage records. As of Summer 2021, we have digitized 65% of our holdings, and are continuing digitization and metadata creation until all historical vital records are made freely available on this website.

Funding for digitization has been supplied under the De Blasio Administration from the Office of Mayoral Budgets.

Phase 1, from 2014-2016, was completed onsite by an external company, eDocNY, overseen by Ken Cobb, Assistant Commissioner. A twenty-person digitization team was complemented by a team working on file naming and quality control. Two office spaces at 31 Chambers Street were converted into high speed digitization labs with coordinated conservation treatments by NYCMA staff. Approximately 8.5 million records were digitized in Phase 1.

Given the enormous scope of the holdings, collaboration was needed to create metadata for the new digital images. The German Genealogy and Italian Genealogical groups generously provided volunteers who created metadata for every record. This index is undergoing quality control and will be integrated into the Historical Vital Records of NYC website soon.

Phase 2 of digitization, for marriage licenses, began in 2018 with an onboarding of staff to allow digitization to continue in-house rather than using an external vendor. A team of four digitization technicians, supervised by Digitization Supervisor Kelli O’Toole, expect to complete digitization in 2024. Planning is underway for creating a metadata index for this set of images.

Finally, Phase 3 of digitization will create high quality images of Manhattan death certificates from original paper records. These records are currently serviced through black & white microfilm copies.

This ongoing mass-digitization project represents a major effort in fulfilling our mission to provide public access to historical records, and is managed by Digital Programs Manager Alexandra Dolan-Mescal and Archives Director Sylvia Kollar. We look forward to providing more features and access to records soon, so please check back regularly for updates.
